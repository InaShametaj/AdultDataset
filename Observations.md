Observations
The Adult dataset is heavily skewed along every protected dimension: approximately two-thirds of records are male, over 85% are White, and the 26–45 age range dominates the sample, which means the baseline logistic regression is effectively optimised for White working-age males and treats all other subgroups as noise to be absorbed into the majority pattern. This imbalance translates directly into a recall gap — the baseline model correctly identifies a substantially larger share of actual high earners among men than among women, and among White individuals than among other racial groups, because it has simply learned to default toward the negative class for underrepresented combinations of (group, positive label). After applying reweighting, recall for women and minority racial groups improves noticeably and the demographic parity gap in positive prediction rates between male and female narrows, while overall accuracy drops by less than one percentage point, confirming that the baseline's accuracy advantage was partly built on systematically under-predicting positive outcomes for minorities. The trade-off also exposes a fundamental tension: reweighting improves demographic parity and equalised opportunity (recall balance) but can slightly worsen precision for the reweighted groups, because pushing the decision boundary toward more positive predictions for a smaller subgroup inevitably introduces some additional false positives — which fairness criterion matters most is ultimately a policy question, not a technical one.
